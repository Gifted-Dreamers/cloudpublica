You're Not Crazy. Here Are the Words for What You're Feeling.

If you know something is deeply wrong but can't quite name it — if you feel stuck between grief and action, between knowing too much and not knowing what to do — this is for you. You're not alone. And there are words for this.

Kristine Socall, MBA International Economic Development

Founder & Executive Director, Gifted Dreamers, Inc. 501(c)(3)

This Is Real. What You're Feeling Makes Sense.

Maybe you're reading this at 2am. Maybe you've been doomscrolling and something in your chest hasn't unclenched in months. Maybe you tried to explain what you're feeling to someone you love and the words came out wrong, or not at all. Maybe you said "I'm fine" again today and meant "I don't know how to describe the shape of what I'm carrying."

Here's the first thing you need to hear: what you're feeling is not a personal failing. It's not anxiety you can meditate away. It's not a problem with your attitude. The weight you're carrying has a source, and that source is not you.

Neuroscience research shows that when we can name a feeling precisely, our brain's threat-response system literally calms down. Psychologist Dan Siegel calls this "name it to tame it" — the act of finding the right word for an emotion activates the prefrontal cortex and dampens the amygdala. The word doesn't fix the problem. But it gives you a handle. It moves you from drowning in a feeling to standing beside it.

So let's try something. Below are the things people have been telling us they feel. If you see yourself in any of these, you're not crazy. You're paying attention.

The Words You've Been Looking For

Each of these starts with what you might say out loud — the raw version. Then comes the structural name: what systems thinkers and researchers call it. These aren't just academic labels. They're proof that what you're experiencing is documented, shared, and understood. You are not imagining this.

"I can't afford anything anymore."
Parameter failure
The numbers — wages, prices, taxes — have been adjusted to serve the powerful. When groceries cost more and your paycheck buys less, that's not inflation happening to you randomly. The parameters of the system have been set against your interests. You're not bad with money. The math was changed.
"I have no cushion. One bad month and I'm done."
Buffer depletion
Buffers are the reserves — savings, sick days, institutional safety nets — that absorb shock. When 60% of Americans live paycheck to paycheck, when federal agencies lose hundreds of thousands of workers overnight, the system's capacity to catch anyone has been deliberately drained. You're not fragile. Your buffers were taken.
"My phone is listening. I don't trust anything digital anymore."
Hostile infrastructure
The digital tools you depend on — your phone, your email, your AI assistant — were built to serve someone else's interests. Courts have ruled AI chats have no legal privilege. Facial recognition is deployed at borders. The infrastructure of daily life has been turned against the people who use it. Your distrust is rational.
"Nothing works fast enough. I voted. I donated. I called. Nothing changed."
Asymmetric delays
The time between democratic action and system response is enormous — and getting longer. Meanwhile, harm happens overnight: a worker fired by algorithm, a court order defied, a community raided at dawn. Fast destruction, slow correction. That's not a bug. It's the architecture. Your frustration is the correct response to a system designed to exhaust you.
"The courts can't stop this. Nobody's being held accountable."
Feedback loop failure
Courts, elections, oversight agencies, the press — these are supposed to be self-correcting mechanisms. When they're systematically weakened or ignored, the system loses its ability to check itself. A federal judge in February 2026 said he was "not aware of another occasion in the history of the United States" where a court had to threaten contempt again and again to force the government to comply. If accountability feels broken, it's because the mechanisms of accountability are being deliberately dismantled.
"Things keep getting worse and I can't figure out why."
Runaway concentration
Wealth concentrates into political power, which writes rules that concentrate more wealth. Surveillance data collected for one purpose gets used for another, which justifies collecting more. These are positive feedback loops — self-reinforcing cycles that accelerate. The feeling that things are speeding up isn't paranoia. It's pattern recognition.
"I can't tell what's real anymore."
Information flow capture
Public information is being choked — press attacked, FOIA requests stonewalled, agency websites scrubbed — while private data is harvested at scale. The people in power are simultaneously increasing their own information access and decreasing yours. Your confusion is manufactured. Deliberately.
"The rules don't apply to them. Only to us."
Impunity
Power operates without consequence while rules are enforced asymmetrically. Executive orders used as props while constitutional limits are violated. The form of law without its substance. You're not being cynical. You're seeing clearly.
"Nobody's coming to save us."
Self-organization suppression
When the system punishes experimentation, surveils organizing, and targets anyone who builds alternatives, it is destroying the conditions for new structures to emerge. But here's the thing the system doesn't want you to know: people are building anyway. Mutual aid networks, community defense, know-your-rights trainings in living rooms, social strikes — self-organization is happening under pressure, right now, in your neighborhood. The system can suppress it. It cannot stop it.
"What are we even fighting for?"
Goal displacement
When a system's stated goals (security, efficiency, freedom) diverge from its actual goals (power preservation, wealth concentration), people lose the thread. The fight feels pointless because the goals have been swapped. Naming this is the first step to articulating what you actually want instead.
"Everything I believed was wrong."
Paradigm collapse
The unstated assumptions that made the system feel legitimate — that the arc of history bends toward justice, that institutions would hold, that playing by the rules would be enough — are visibly failing. This isn't disillusionment. It's seeing more clearly. And it hurts, because it means the ground you built your life on was partly mythological. Grief for a world that maybe never quite existed the way you believed it did — that's real grief. It deserves to be honored, not dismissed.
"I'm paralyzed. I don't know where to start."
Paradigm rigidity
The inability to act without a totalizing theory. Waiting for the "right answer" before doing anything. Research on learned helplessness shows this is neurological, not moral — the brain's anxiety-regulation mechanisms shut down under sustained, uncontrollable stress. You're not lazy. Your nervous system is in shutdown mode because it has been overwhelmed. The way out isn't to think harder. It's to move smaller.
"The grief you feel about the world is evidence of how much you care.
That's not weakness. That's the entry point."

You Are Not Alone in This

Not metaphorically. Actually.

If you're reading this, you're probably someone who lies awake at night thinking about things most people avoid. You probably feel like you're carrying knowledge that you can't put down and can't share, because the people around you either don't see it yet or have decided not to look. You probably wonder sometimes if you're the problem.

You're not the problem. And there are more people who feel exactly what you feel than you've been led to believe.

Sociologist Émile Durkheim described something he called collective effervescence — the moment when people recognize they share an experience, especially an intense or difficult one, and something shifts. The private burden becomes communal. This is not just comfort. It is genuinely energizing. It is the feeling underneath every movement that has ever changed anything: we see the same thing, and we are going to do something about it together.

The system depends on your isolation. It depends on you believing you are the only one awake at 2am, the only one who noticed, the only one who cares. That belief is the most effective tool of control there is. Not surveillance. Not censorship. Loneliness. The manufactured conviction that you are alone.

You are not alone.

What You Can Actually Do

Writer Rebecca Solnit reminds us that hope is not optimism. Hope lives in uncertainty — and uncertainty means the outcome is not fixed. The future is genuinely open. "We can't know what will happen. That's not a reason for despair — it's the exact condition that makes your action matter."

Joanna Macy, who has spent decades studying how people move from paralysis to action, offers a simple framework: we are allowed to grieve and still go forward. These are not opposites. Grief for what is being lost, and commitment to what can still be built — these live in the same heart, at the same time.

adrienne maree brown writes: "What happens between you and one other person is a rehearsal for the world you want." Change doesn't need to be massive to be real. The small shifts you make in how you show up — those compound.

So here is not a ten-step plan. Here is what is actually within your reach:

  • Name it. You just did, by reading this far. You now have words for what you're carrying. Use them. Say them out loud to someone you trust. "I think what I'm feeling is paradigm collapse" might sound strange, but it beats "I don't know what's wrong with me."
  • Find your people. Not allies in the abstract. Actual humans in your neighborhood, your workplace, your congregation, your group chat. The ones who will show up. The ones who are also awake at 2am. You probably already know who they are.
  • Start smaller than you think you should. Your nervous system is in protection mode. Don't fight that. Honor it. One conversation. One meeting. One small act of care for someone near you. The mycelium theory of change: what grows underground, invisibly, is what eventually transforms the forest floor.
  • Protect yourself. Learn about data sovereignty. Understand the legal risks of your digital life. Build your emergency buffers. These aren't paranoid acts. They're the equivalent of putting on your own oxygen mask first.
  • Refuse the premise that you have to fix everything alone. You don't need to be a lawyer, an activist, or a tech person. You need to be a human who cares and who shows up. That is enough. That is more than enough.
  • Let grief and hope coexist. You don't have to choose. The grief is proof of love. The hope is proof of vision. Carry both.
"How do I go from reading a blog post at 2am with my heart racing
to actually being part of something?"
You just started.

Why Naming This Matters

Systems thinker Donella Meadows spent her career studying where to intervene in complex systems. Her insight: most activism and most political debate operate at the lowest-leverage points — arguing over numbers, tweaking parameters, fighting about this policy or that policy. Meanwhile, the system's actual dysfunction lives at the highest-leverage points: who has information, who writes the rules, whether people can self-organize, what the system is actually optimized for, and what assumptions we hold about how the world works.

The words above — parameter failure, buffer depletion, hostile infrastructure, asymmetric delays, feedback loop failure, runaway concentration, information flow capture, impunity, self-organization suppression, goal displacement, paradigm collapse, paradigm rigidity — are not just vocabulary. They are a map. They show you that what feels like personal chaos is structural. They show you where the system is actually broken. And they show you where intervention actually matters.

Most people get stuck at "raise the minimum wage" or "vote them out in 2028." Those matter. But the changes that actually transform systems happen when people change who has information, change who writes the rules, enable self-organization, redefine what we're optimizing for, and shift the underlying story about how the world works.

That's what you're doing when you name the problem. You're not just processing your feelings. You're shifting the paradigm. You're giving language to something millions of people feel but couldn't articulate. And language is how movements begin.

Name the paradigm that's failing. Name the goal that should replace it. The rest follows.

A Final Word

If you found yourself in these words — if something unclenched even slightly — share this with someone who needs it. Not as homework. As an act of solidarity. As a way of saying: I see you. I feel it too. We're going to figure this out.

The word that ties it all together is agency. The crisis we're living through is fundamentally a crisis of agency — the systematic erosion of people's ability to act on their own behalf. Everything on this site exists to restore that agency, one person, one family, one neighborhood at a time.

You're reading this because something in you hasn't given up. That something is not naive. It's not foolish. It's the most important thing about you.

Keep going.
